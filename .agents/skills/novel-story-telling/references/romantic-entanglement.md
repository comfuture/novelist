## Safety And Genre Contract

Use desire, jealousy, betrayal, status, secrecy, and emotional excess to intensify relational choices. Do not treat jealousy as proof of love. Do not romanticize surveillance, coercion, isolation, sexual violence, or physical violence without agency and consequence.

Make every participant a character with an independent desire and moral responsibility. A rival is not a prop.

## Relationship Models

### Love components

Track intimacy, passion, and commitment separately for every important bond. Conflict becomes specific when partners value different components, promise one while offering another, or disagree about what has been exchanged.

### Threat triangle

Track:

- threatened person;
- valued partner, bond, or status;
- real, potential, remembered, or imagined rival.

The rival may be an ex-partner, friend, family member, child, career, social class, deceased lover, ambition, or idealized past.

Separate relationship threat from self-worth threat. The same observation can provoke fear of abandonment, humiliation, loss of status, or proof of personal inadequacy. Give tactics to the specific fear.

## Recommended Flow

1. **Unequal bond:** establish imbalance in intimacy, passion, commitment, status, sacrifice, or public recognition.
2. **Threat signal:** reallocate attention, confidence, time, money, protection, or future commitment ambiguously but consequentially.
3. **Private interpretation:** connect the signal to a personal wound or status fear.
4. **Protective tactic:** seek reassurance, proximity, information, leverage, retaliation, or control; let the tactic damage trust.
5. **Triangle activation:** give the third party an independent goal and decision.
6. **Reciprocal concealment:** let one person hide an innocent fact to avoid conflict and another hide a self-serving fact, making concealments difficult to distinguish.
7. **Public or irreversible escalation:** exposure, engagement, financial transfer, family intervention, leaked message, career sacrifice, public humiliation, or violence changes what repair can mean.
8. **Truth and reversal:** separate objective event, believed event, and manipulation. Do not let factual innocence erase relational harm.
9. **Moral choice:** choose trust, confession, retaliation, possession, sacrifice, separation, or self-respect.
10. **Aftermath:** resolve the arrangement and the underlying need for worth, certainty, belonging, or control.

## Escalation Devices

- **Ambiguous intimacy:** private joke, confidence, protected secret, or unexplained familiarity.
- **Commitment asymmetry:** one bond is private while another is public.
- **Resource transfer:** time, money, inheritance, protection, housing, opportunity, or legitimacy flows toward a rival.
- **Retroactive jealousy:** new history changes the meaning of the present.
- **Strategic reassurance:** affection calms suspicion while avoiding truth.
- **Jealousy induction:** visible closeness is used deliberately to regain power.
- **Proxy rivalry:** family, children, friends, or institutions compete through the couple.
- **Double standard:** transparency is demanded but not reciprocated.
- **Witnessed fragment:** a true detail is observed without causal context.
- **Proof contamination:** forged evidence is mixed with real concealment.
- **Public claim:** status is asserted before private agreement.
- **Sacrifice competition:** care and sacrifice become moral debt.

Escalate through intimacy, public status, resource allocation, evidence, and irreversible conduct, not through repetitive shouting.

## Scene Devices

- A dinner or ceremony where seating, names, and introductions declare status.
- An object with conflicting histories: ring, photograph, key, message, receipt, or medical record.
- A private conversation overheard only in part, with a causal reason for limited access.
- Reassurance whose word choice reveals a hidden assumption.
- A rival scene without the shared love interest, exposing independent goals.
- A care scene where illness or crisis reveals actual priority.
- A confrontation with three versions of one event.
- Public humiliation followed by private reversal of power.
- An ordinary domestic task performed with changed intimacy after betrayal.

## Resolution

- Distinguish factual innocence from relational responsibility.
- Require repair proportionate to the breach: disclosure, relinquished leverage, public correction, restitution, boundary change, or separation.
- Let a character reject possession as a substitute for intimacy.
- Permit unresolved desire with resolved conduct.
- In tragedy, show the causal chain from threat appraisal to destructive tactic to irreversible loss.
- Do not use sudden death solely to avoid choosing among relationships.
- Show the new relationship state through access, habit, trust, resources, and public status.

## Failure Modes

- Jealousy is treated as proof of love.
- Coercion or violence is rewarded without consequence.
- A rival has no independent agency.
- One low-risk question could end every misunderstanding.
- Facts change retroactively only to prolong the triangle.
- Gender stereotypes replace character-specific fear.
- Factual innocence instantly restores trust after manipulation.
- Every conflict becomes a shouting scene.
- Sensational escalation ignores emotional residue.
- A character's stated boundary changes without an event or decision.
